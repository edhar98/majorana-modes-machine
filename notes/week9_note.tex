\section{Purpose and Context}

Week 8 opened Block 4 with a \emph{frozen-state} noise diagnostic in \texttt{src/block4.py}. There, the validated even-parity ground state $\rho_0(\mu)=\ket{\psi_+(\mu)}\bra{\psi_+(\mu)}$ was held fixed, and noise was applied directly to that density matrix: a hand-rolled two-qubit depolarizing channel on nearest-neighbor pairs, a symmetric readout attenuation $(1-2\epsilon)^L$, and finite-shot sampling. Crucially, that channel was applied \emph{once per pair}, independent of any circuit.

Real hardware noise does not work that way. On a NISQ device, error enters \emph{through the gates}. Every entangling operation in the ansatz injects a fresh error channel, so the total corruption grows with the gate count and the circuit depth. Week 9 asks how to model this correctly with Qiskit's noise stack, and how the practical API connects to the underlying channel theory.

This note has two goals:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Theory.} Treat a noisy gate as a completely positive trace-preserving (CPTP) map composed with the ideal unitary, write the depolarizing and thermal-relaxation channels in Kraus/Pauli form, and pin down the conventions.
  \item \textbf{Practice.} Build a Qiskit \texttt{NoiseModel}, attach gate errors to specific instructions, read out the edge-string observable from a density-matrix simulation, and prove that the Week 8 hand-rolled channel is exactly a special case of Qiskit's \texttt{depolarizing\_error}.
\end{enumerate}

All conventions follow the existing repository: the little-endian Jordan--Wigner qubit Hamiltonian and the edge-string operator $O_{\mathrm{edge}}=\Oedge$ from \texttt{block3\_core.py}.

\section{Gate Errors as Quantum Channels (Theory)}

\subsection{The gate-then-error model}

An ideal gate acts by unitary conjugation,
\[
  \mathcal{U}_g(\rho)=U_g\,\rho\,U_g^\dagger .
\]
A noisy gate is modeled as the ideal unitary followed by an error channel $\mathcal{E}_g$,
\[
  \tilde{\mathcal{U}}_g=\mathcal{E}_g\circ\mathcal{U}_g ,
\qquad
  \mathcal{E}_g(\rho)=\sum_k K_k\,\rho\,K_k^\dagger ,
\qquad
  \sum_k K_k^\dagger K_k = I ,
\]
where the $\{K_k\}$ are Kraus operators and the completeness relation guarantees the map is CPTP (it preserves $\Tr\rho=1$ and positivity). This is precisely the model Qiskit Aer implements: for every instruction in the \emph{transpiled} circuit, the associated error channel is applied immediately after that instruction.

For a circuit $U=U_{g_M}\cdots U_{g_1}$, the noisy evolution of the input state is the full composition
\[
  \rho_{\mathrm{out}}
  =\bigl(\mathcal{E}_{g_M}\circ\mathcal{U}_{g_M}\bigr)\circ\cdots\circ
   \bigl(\mathcal{E}_{g_1}\circ\mathcal{U}_{g_1}\bigr)(\rho_{\mathrm{in}}).
\]
The central consequence, and the qualitative difference from Week 8, is that \textbf{noise scales with gate count and depth}. A single channel applied once to a frozen state cannot capture this; a gate-level \texttt{NoiseModel} can.

\subsection{Physical origin: from Lindblad to Pauli channels}

These error channels are not arbitrary. A Markovian open system obeys the Lindblad master equation
\[
  \dot{\rho}=-i[H,\rho]+\sum_k\Bigl(L_k\rho L_k^\dagger-\tfrac12\{L_k^\dagger L_k,\rho\}\Bigr).
\]
Integrating over a finite gate duration $\tau$ produces a CPTP map, i.e.\ a Kraus channel. Isotropic, fully symmetric noise integrates to the \emph{depolarizing} channel; energy relaxation $\ket{1}\to\ket{0}$ integrates to \emph{amplitude damping} ($T_1$); pure loss of phase coherence integrates to \emph{phase damping} ($T_2$). So the practical Qiskit channels below are the gate-duration-integrated shadows of physical decoherence, which is why they are parameterized by either an error probability or by $(T_1,T_2,\tau)$.

\section{The Depolarizing Channel and Two Conventions}

\subsection{Definitions and the twirl identity}

The $n$-qubit depolarizing channel mixes the state toward the maximally mixed state. The key algebraic fact is the \emph{Pauli twirl identity}: averaging $\rho$ over all $4^n$ Pauli strings $P$ (including the identity) gives the maximally mixed state,
\begin{equation}
  \frac{1}{4^n}\sum_{P}P\,\rho\,P^\dagger=\frac{\Tr\rho}{2^n}\,I .
  \label{eq:twirl}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Qiskit convention (replace-with-mixed)}

Qiskit's \texttt{depolarizing\_error(p, n)} uses the ``with probability $p$, replace by the maximally mixed state'' convention:
\[
  \mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{Qiskit}}_p(\rho)
  =(1-p)\,\rho+p\,\frac{\Tr\rho}{2^n}\,I .
\]
Substituting the twirl identity~\eqref{eq:twirl} and separating the identity term,
\begin{align}
  \mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{Qiskit}}_p(\rho)
  &=(1-p)\,\rho+\frac{p}{4^n}\sum_{P}P\rho P^\dagger \nonumber\\
  &=\Bigl(1-p+\tfrac{p}{4^n}\Bigr)\rho
    +\frac{p}{4^n}\sum_{P\neq I}P\rho P^\dagger
  =\Bigl(1-p\,\tfrac{4^n-1}{4^n}\Bigr)\rho
    +\frac{p}{4^n}\sum_{P\neq I}P\rho P^\dagger .
  \label{eq:qiskit}
\end{align}

\subsection{Week 8 convention (random non-identity Pauli)}

The hand-rolled channel in \texttt{block4.py} uses the ``with probability $q\equiv p_{2q}$, apply a uniformly random \emph{non-identity} Pauli'' convention. For a two-qubit pair, with $4^2-1=15$ non-identity Paulis,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{D}_q(\rho)=(1-q)\,\rho+\frac{q}{4^n-1}\sum_{P\neq I}P\rho P^\dagger .
  \label{eq:block4}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Matching the two conventions}

Equations~\eqref{eq:qiskit} and~\eqref{eq:block4} are the \emph{same} channel for a suitable parameter map. Match the non-identity coefficient first:
\[
  \frac{p}{4^n}=\frac{q}{4^n-1}
  \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
  \boxed{\,p=\frac{4^n}{4^n-1}\,q\,}.
\]
The identity coefficient then matches automatically:
\[
  1-p\,\frac{4^n-1}{4^n}
  =1-\frac{4^n}{4^n-1}\,q\cdot\frac{4^n-1}{4^n}
  =1-q ,
\]
which is exactly the identity weight in~\eqref{eq:block4}. So no separate condition is needed; the single map $p=\tfrac{4^n}{4^n-1}q$ makes the two channels identical.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ccc}
\toprule
Qubits in channel & $4^n$ & Qiskit parameter $p$ for Week 8 $q$ \\
\midrule
$n=1$ & $4$  & $p=\tfrac{4}{3}\,q\approx1.333\,q$ \\
$n=2$ & $16$ & $p=\tfrac{16}{15}\,q\approx1.067\,q$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The endpoints are also consistent: $q=1$ in~\eqref{eq:block4} (always apply a random non-identity Pauli) maps to $p=\tfrac{4^n}{4^n-1}$, which is precisely the maximal valid Qiskit value where the identity weight $1-q$ vanishes and the channel is fully depolarizing.

\paragraph{Numerical verification.} Constructing both channels as superoperators and acting on a random two-qubit density matrix, the mapped parameter $p=\tfrac{16}{15}\,q$ reproduces the Week 8 channel to machine precision ($\max$ elementwise difference $\sim10^{-16}$). Using the naive guess $p=q$ instead leaves a real discrepancy of $\sim8\times10^{-4}$ at $q=0.07$. The factor $\tfrac{16}{15}$ is therefore physically required, not cosmetic, and any quantitative comparison between the Week 8 plot and a Qiskit \texttt{NoiseModel} must apply it. This check is implemented as \texttt{verify\_channel\_equivalence} in \texttt{src/block4.py} and runs as part of \texttt{python block4.py --plots 2}, where it reports $1.4\times10^{-16}$ for the mapped parameter against $7.9\times10^{-4}$ for the naive one.

\section{Thermal Relaxation as a Gate Error ($T_1$/$T_2$)}

Depolarizing noise is symmetric and structureless; it is the right first check but not the real physics. The realistic upgrade attaches a \emph{thermal relaxation} channel that depends on the gate duration $\tau$ and the device coherence times.

Qiskit provides \texttt{thermal\_relaxation\_error(t1, t2, time)}. Schematically it combines:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Amplitude damping} ($T_1$): the excitation-loss probability over the gate time is $p_{T_1}=1-e^{-\tau/T_1}$, with Kraus operators that map $\ket{1}\to\ket{0}$.
  \item \textbf{Phase damping} ($T_2$): loss of off-diagonal coherence with rate set by $T_2$.
\end{itemize}
Physical consistency requires
\[
  T_2\le 2T_1 ,
\]
since dephasing cannot be slower than the limit set by relaxation alone. Qiskit enforces this and raises an error otherwise.

\paragraph{Why this matters for the Majorana diagnostic.} Two effects are qualitatively distinct from depolarizing contrast loss:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{$T_1$ is parity poisoning.} In the Jordan--Wigner encoding, qubit occupation tracks fermion occupation. An amplitude-damping event $\ket{1}\to\ket{0}$ changes occupation and can flip the fermion parity, pushing the state out of the even sector that the edge-string diagnostic lives in. This is the relaxation mechanism flagged as ``not implemented yet'' in the Week 8 note.
  \item \textbf{$T_2$ kills non-local coherence.} The edge string $O_{\mathrm{edge}}=\Oedge$ is a correlator between the two chain ends. Pure dephasing suppresses exactly the off-diagonal coherence that carries that endpoint-to-endpoint correlation, so the Majorana signal can collapse even while local populations look plausible.
\end{itemize}
Because thermal relaxation is gate-duration-dependent and two-qubit gates are the longest and noisiest, the $(T_1,T_2,\tau)$ model also justifies, from first principles, the Week 8 modeling choice of putting the dominant noise on the two-qubit nearest-neighbor operations.

\section{Building a NoiseModel in Qiskit (Practice)}

The practical workflow has three pieces: construct the error channels, attach them to gate instructions, and read out the observable from a density-matrix simulation.

\subsection{Construct and attach errors}

\begin{verbatim}
from qiskit_aer.noise import (NoiseModel, depolarizing_error,
                              thermal_relaxation_error)

p2q = 0.07
noise_model = NoiseModel()

err_2q = depolarizing_error(16/15 * p2q, 2)        # Week 8 -> Qiskit map
noise_model.add_all_qubit_quantum_error(err_2q, ['cx'])

# realistic alternative: relaxation on every two-qubit gate
err_relax = thermal_relaxation_error(t1=80e3, t2=60e3, time=300)
err_relax_2q = err_relax.tensor(err_relax)
noise_model.add_all_qubit_quantum_error(err_relax_2q, ['cx'])
\end{verbatim}

\texttt{add\_all\_qubit\_quantum\_error} applies the channel after \emph{every} occurrence of the named gate. To localize noise to specific lattice pairs instead, use \texttt{noise\_model.add\_quantum\_error(err\_2q, ['cx'], [a, b])} for the pair $(a,b)$.

\paragraph{The transpilation gotcha.} Errors attach to gate \emph{names}, and a noise model only fires on names that survive transpilation into the simulator basis. On this environment the model reports its basis as \texttt{['cx', 'id', 'rz', 'sx']}; an error attached to \texttt{cx} fires only because \texttt{cx} is preserved. If the target basis used \texttt{ecr} or \texttt{cz}, an error attached to \texttt{cx} would never trigger. Always attach errors to the gate names that the circuit is actually transpiled into, and transpile against the noisy backend.

\subsection{Read out the edge string from a density matrix}

\begin{verbatim}
import numpy as np
from qiskit import transpile
from qiskit.quantum_info import SparsePauliOp
from qiskit_aer import AerSimulator

sim = AerSimulator(method='density_matrix', noise_model=noise_model)

qc = ansatz.assign_parameters(theta).copy()   # the validated VQE state prep
qc.save_density_matrix()

rho = sim.run(transpile(qc, sim)).result().data()['density_matrix']

O_edge = SparsePauliOp(['XZZX'], [1.0])        # L = 4 edge string
s = float(np.real(rho.expectation_value(O_edge)))
\end{verbatim}

This pattern was confirmed to run on Qiskit~2.4.2 / Aer~0.17.2. For a shot-based readout instead of an exact trace, use the Aer \texttt{Estimator} primitive with the same \texttt{noise\_model} and a finite \texttt{shots} setting, which reproduces the binomial sampling layer used in Week 8.

\section{Reproducing Week 8 from a Circuit (Bridge)}

The two pictures are the same physics. The Week 8 composition
\[
  \rho_{\mathrm{gate}}
  =\mathcal{D}_{L-2,L-1}\circ\cdots\circ\mathcal{D}_{1,2}\circ\mathcal{D}_{0,1}(\rho_0)
\]
is reproduced inside Qiskit by initializing the simulator in the frozen state $\rho_0$ and applying one depolarizing channel per nearest-neighbor pair with the mapped parameter:
\begin{verbatim}
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit.quantum_info import DensityMatrix

err = depolarizing_error(16/15 * p2q, 2)
qc = QuantumCircuit(L)
qc.set_density_matrix(DensityMatrix(rho0))       # frozen even-parity state
for site in range(L - 1):
    qc.append(err.to_instruction(), [site, site + 1])
qc.save_density_matrix()
\end{verbatim}
Because the superoperators are identical (verified above to $10^{-16}$), this returns the same $\Tr[O_{\mathrm{edge}}\,\rho_{\mathrm{gate}}]$ as \texttt{apply\_gate\_noise} in \texttt{block4.py}. The only difference between Week 8 and Week 9 is bookkeeping: Week 8 applies the channel by hand once per pair on a frozen state, while the \texttt{NoiseModel} applies it automatically wherever the corresponding gate appears in the real circuit.

\section{Why the Week 8 Model Cannot Represent Real Gate Noise}

The frozen-state test of Week 8 is a useful isolation experiment, but it is \emph{structurally} not a model of hardware gate noise. Two independent reasons make this precise.

\paragraph{1. It is depth-blind.} On hardware the state is built by a circuit, and every two-qubit gate carries its own error channel. A linear-entanglement \texttt{EfficientSU2} ansatz with $r$ repetitions contains $r(L-1)$ CNOTs, so the number of noise insertions grows with depth. The Week 8 model instead applies exactly one channel per nearest-neighbor pair, $L-1$ in total, \emph{independent of how the state was prepared}. It therefore cannot represent a deeper circuit and systematically under-counts the noise.

\paragraph{2. It applies noise to the wrong state, in the wrong order.} Real errors occur \emph{between} the gates and are then propagated and scrambled by every subsequent unitary. Week 8 instead applies its channels once, at the very end, to the already-perfect state. Because a channel does not commute with a unitary, $\mathcal{E}\circ\mathcal{U}\neq\mathcal{U}\circ\mathcal{E}$, the end-applied result differs from the interleaved one even at matched channel strength and matched count.

Both models start from the same well-prepared state (fidelity $\approx0.999$, ideal $|\langle O_{\mathrm{edge}}\rangle|\approx1$). The Week 8 estimate is essentially \emph{flat} at $\approx0.85$ across all depths, because it always applies the same $L-1=3$ channels. The circuit-level estimate decays \emph{monotonically} from $0.75$ to $0.51$ as the circuit deepens from $6$ to $15$ CNOTs, and the trace distance between the two noisy density matrices grows from $0.11$ to $0.32$. At depth $r=5$ the Week 8 model reports a surviving signal of $0.86$ while the real circuit produces $0.51$: an over-estimate of roughly $70\%$.

The circuit-level decimals correspond to one VQE optimum. Because the depolarizing channel acts on the realized rotation angles, and \texttt{EfficientSU2} can represent the same fidelity-$1$ state with different angles, a different optimizer run or library version shifts the exact circuit values (the Week 8 column, which acts on the prepared state once, and all qualitative trends are unaffected). \paragraph{Conclusion.} The Week 8 frozen-state diagnostic is an \emph{optimistic bound}. It answers a narrow question --- ``if the perfect state were available, could a single round of measurement-level noise still be read out?'' --- and it is useful precisely because it isolates the measurement layer. But it is not, and cannot be, a faithful model of what a real device produces: real noise accumulates with circuit depth and is interleaved with the gates, and the demonstration above shows the two pictures diverge without bound as the circuit grows. The circuit-level \texttt{NoiseModel} is the correct tool for the realistic NISQ picture; the Week 8 number should be read only as a best case, never as the hardware expectation.

This also makes quantitative the trade-off that Week 7's depth diagnostic only hinted at: a deeper \texttt{EfficientSU2} ansatz buys expressibility and lower energy error (the ideal column stays near $1$), but each repetition adds a layer of \texttt{cx} gates, and every \texttt{cx} now carries an error channel, so the realized edge-string contrast falls with depth.

\section{Connection to the Majorana Edge-String Diagnostic}

The edge string is the most exposed observable in this whole program because it is non-local. Its value depends on a coherent correlation built across the chain by a ladder of two-qubit gates. In the circuit-level model:
\begin{itemize}
  \item every noisy \texttt{cx} injects depolarization that pulls the endpoint-to-endpoint coherence toward zero, suppressing $|\langle O_{\mathrm{edge}}\rangle|$ even when local observables remain reasonable;
  \item $T_1$ relaxation can poison the parity sector, which is qualitatively worse than contrast loss because it moves the state into the wrong physical sector;
  \item the transition regions near $|\mu|\approx2t$ are the most fragile, since the ideal signal is already changing rapidly there and needs little noise to blur the apparent phase boundary.
\end{itemize}

\section{Gate-Error Verification and Topological Protection}

Plot~3 of \texttt{block4.py} combines two checks in one figure (Fig.~\ref{fig:protection}).

\paragraph{Verification across the phase diagram.} The left panel sweeps $\mu$ over $[-3.5t, 3.5t]$ on the frozen even-parity state and applies the verified gate channel two independent ways at $p_{\mathrm{cx}}=0.10$: the analytic \texttt{apply\_gate\_noise} (solid line) and Qiskit's \texttt{depolarizing\_error} evolved as a superoperator on each nearest-neighbor pair (open markers). The two coincide to $\max|\,\cdot\,|=3.3\times10^{-16}$ across \emph{every} $\mu$, not just at a single density matrix. The gate error is therefore correctly integrated over the entire diagnostic, with the $\tfrac{16}{15}$ parameter map applied.

\paragraph{How topology protects.} The right panel shot-samples the same verified channel at increasing strength $p_{\mathrm{cx}}\in\{0.05, 0.10, 0.20\}$. The edge-string signal is the measurable proxy for the Majorana edge mode, which exists only in the topological region $|\mu|<2t$ (shaded) and is protected there by the bulk gap. Three features express that protection:
\begin{itemize}
  \item the signal is concentrated inside the topological window and is largest at the $\mu=0$ sweet spot, where the gap is maximal and the prepared state is simplest;
  \item under noise the topological bump is suppressed in amplitude but \emph{not} in location: it stays well above the trivial floor, so the topological and trivial phases remain distinguishable;
  \item the transitions $|\mu|=2t$, where the gap closes, are the fragile points --- the ideal signal is already small there, and noise blurs the apparent boundary most easily.
\end{itemize}
A careful statement is required here: decoherence itself is \emph{not} suppressed by topology. Without active error correction, gate noise always reduces contrast, as the right panel shows. What the gap protects is the \emph{existence and localization} of the edge mode; the demonstration then shows that this protected signature survives moderate noise as a still-measurable phase distinction, most robustly deep in the topological phase. This is the sense in which topology helps a NISQ measurement of the Kitaev chain.

\section{What This Enables Next}

With gate errors integrated at the circuit level, the remaining open pieces from the Week 8 program become concrete next steps:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Noisy VQE optimization.} Run the optimizer (COBYLA/SPSA) using a \texttt{NoiseModel}-backed Aer \texttt{Estimator}, so every cost evaluation is corrupted and the optimizer must search through noise rather than receiving a frozen validated state.
  \item \textbf{Backend-calibrated noise.} Use \texttt{NoiseModel.from\_backend(...)} to replace the hand-set parameters with real device $T_1$/$T_2$ and gate-error data.
  \item \textbf{Readout/SPAM layer.} Fold the asymmetric assignment-matrix readout model from the Week 8 theory into the same \texttt{NoiseModel}, unifying gate noise and measurement noise in one circuit-level simulation.
\end{itemize}
