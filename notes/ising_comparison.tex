\section{Purpose}

The Jordan-Wigner mapping turns the Kitaev chain into a spin-chain Hamiltonian.
At first sight this can look similar to an Ising model, especially at the
sweet spot $t=\Delta$, where only one two-qubit coupling remains. These notes
clarify the relationship between a standard antiferromagnetic Ising model and
the qubit Hamiltonian used in this project.

The main message is:
\begin{quote}
  The Kitaev-chain qubit Hamiltonian can be written as an Ising-like spin
  model, but its physical interpretation is not the same as an ordinary
  antiferromagnet. The spin variables are a fermionic encoding, and the
  topological information appears most naturally in parity sectors and
  non-local strings.
\end{quote}

\section{Background: Ising Spins, Operators, and Eigenstates}

The Ising model starts from a very simple local degree of freedom: each site has
a two-state variable. In the classical model this variable is written as
\begin{equation}
  S_j = \pm 1.
\end{equation}
One may call $S_j=+1$ ``spin up'' and $S_j=-1$ ``spin down'', but the important
point is only that every site has two possible values.

In the quantum version, the two states at site $j$ become a two-dimensional
Hilbert space. We usually choose the $Z$ basis
\begin{equation}
  \ket{\uparrow}_j = \ket{0}_j,
  \qquad
  \ket{\downarrow}_j = \ket{1}_j,
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
  Z_j\ket{\uparrow}_j=+\ket{\uparrow}_j,
  \qquad
  Z_j\ket{\downarrow}_j=-\ket{\downarrow}_j.
\end{equation}
Thus the classical variable $S_j$ is represented by the eigenvalue of the Pauli
operator $Z_j$:
\begin{equation}
  S_j \longleftrightarrow Z_j.
\end{equation}

If we use physical spin-$\frac{1}{2}$ operators, they are related to Pauli
matrices by
\begin{equation}
  S_j^\alpha = \frac{\hbar}{2}\sigma_j^\alpha,
  \qquad \alpha=x,y,z.
\end{equation}
In quantum-information notation we usually set $\hbar=1$ and write the
Hamiltonian directly with Pauli matrices $X,Y,Z$. This absorbs factors of
$1/2$ into the coupling constants. For example,
\begin{equation}
  J_z S_j^z S_{j+1}^z
  = \frac{J_z\hbar^2}{4} Z_jZ_{j+1}.
\end{equation}
So when these notes write $JZ_jZ_{j+1}$, $J$ is the effective Pauli-basis
coupling, not necessarily the microscopic spin-coupling constant before the
$S^z=(\hbar/2)Z$ conversion.
For a chain of $L$ spins, the computational basis states are tensor products
\begin{equation}
  \ket{s_0s_1\cdots s_{L-1}}
  = \ket{s_0}\otimes\ket{s_1}\otimes\cdots\otimes\ket{s_{L-1}},
\end{equation}
where each $s_j$ is either $\uparrow$ or $\downarrow$.

\subsection{Two-spin bond energies}

The elementary Ising interaction is one bond:
\begin{equation}
  H_{j,j+1}=J Z_jZ_{j+1}.
\end{equation}
Because $Z_jZ_{j+1}$ is diagonal in the $Z$ basis, the four two-spin basis
states are already eigenstates:
\begin{align}
  Z_jZ_{j+1}\ket{\uparrow\uparrow} &= +\ket{\uparrow\uparrow}, \\
  Z_jZ_{j+1}\ket{\downarrow\downarrow} &= +\ket{\downarrow\downarrow}, \\
  Z_jZ_{j+1}\ket{\uparrow\downarrow} &= -\ket{\uparrow\downarrow}, \\
  Z_jZ_{j+1}\ket{\downarrow\uparrow} &= -\ket{\downarrow\uparrow}.
\end{align}
Therefore the bond energies are
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ccc}
\toprule
State & $Z_jZ_{j+1}$ eigenvalue & Bond energy \\
\midrule
$\ket{\uparrow\uparrow}$ & $+1$ & $+J$ \\
$\ket{\downarrow\downarrow}$ & $+1$ & $+J$ \\
$\ket{\uparrow\downarrow}$ & $-1$ & $-J$ \\
$\ket{\downarrow\uparrow}$ & $-1$ & $-J$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
For an antiferromagnet, $J>0$, so the lower-energy bond states are
\begin{equation}
  \ket{\uparrow\downarrow},\qquad \ket{\downarrow\uparrow}.
\end{equation}
For a ferromagnet, $J<0$, so the lower-energy bond states are
\begin{equation}
  \ket{\uparrow\uparrow},\qquad \ket{\downarrow\downarrow}.
\end{equation}
This sign convention is why the antiferromagnetic Ising model is usually written
with $J>0$ in $J\sum Z_jZ_{j+1}$.

\subsection{Many-spin eigenstates without a transverse field}

For
\begin{equation}
  H_0=J\sum_{j=0}^{L-2}Z_jZ_{j+1},
\end{equation}
every computational-basis product state is an eigenstate, because all bond
operators commute:
\begin{equation}
  [Z_jZ_{j+1},Z_kZ_{k+1}]=0.
\end{equation}
If a configuration has $N_{\mathrm{same}}$ aligned bonds and
$N_{\mathrm{opp}}$ opposite bonds, its energy is
\begin{equation}
  E=J(N_{\mathrm{same}}-N_{\mathrm{opp}}),
  \qquad
  N_{\mathrm{same}}+N_{\mathrm{opp}}=L-1.
\end{equation}
For $J>0$, minimizing the energy means maximizing $N_{\mathrm{opp}}$.
On an open chain this gives the two staggered ground states
\begin{equation}
  \ket{\uparrow\downarrow\uparrow\downarrow\cdots},
  \qquad
  \ket{\downarrow\uparrow\downarrow\uparrow\cdots},
\end{equation}
with ground-state energy
\begin{equation}
  E_0=-J(L-1).
\end{equation}
A domain wall is a local defect where one bond fails to alternate; each such
bad bond changes its contribution from $-J$ to $+J$, costing
\begin{equation}
  \Delta E_{\text{bad bond}}=2J.
\end{equation}

\subsection{What the transverse field changes}

The transverse-field Ising model adds a term such as
\begin{equation}
  -h\sum_j X_j.
\end{equation}
This term is not diagonal in the $Z$ basis, because
\begin{equation}
  X_j\ket{\uparrow}_j=\ket{\downarrow}_j,
  \qquad
  X_j\ket{\downarrow}_j=\ket{\uparrow}_j.
\end{equation}
Therefore the product states that were exact eigenstates of $H_0$ are no longer
exact eigenstates when $h\neq 0$. The transverse field flips spins and creates
quantum superpositions of different classical configurations. The competition
between $J$ and $h$ is the source of the quantum phase transition.

\section{Reference Model: The Antiferromagnetic Ising Chain}

A classical nearest-neighbor Ising chain is often written as
\begin{equation}
  H_{\mathrm{Ising}}
  = J\sum_{j=0}^{L-2} S_j S_{j+1},
  \qquad S_j=\pm 1.
\end{equation}
For $J>0$, neighboring spins prefer opposite signs:
\begin{equation}
  S_jS_{j+1}=-1.
\end{equation}
Thus the two classical ground-state patterns are the two staggered
configurations
\begin{equation}
  (+,-,+,-,\ldots), \qquad (-,+,-,+,\ldots).
\end{equation}
The order parameter is local after including the alternating sign:
\begin{equation}
  m_{\mathrm{stag}}
  = \frac{1}{L}\sum_{j=0}^{L-1}(-1)^j S_j.
\end{equation}

The quantum transverse-field antiferromagnetic Ising chain is commonly written
as
\begin{equation}
  H_{\mathrm{AFI}}
  = J\sum_{j=0}^{L-2} Z_jZ_{j+1}
    - h\sum_{j=0}^{L-1} X_j,
  \qquad J>0.
\end{equation}
Here the $Z_jZ_{j+1}$ term favors staggered $Z$-basis order, while the
transverse field $X_j$ creates quantum fluctuations. The transition is between
an ordered antiferromagnetic phase and a disordered paramagnetic phase. In this
model, the natural diagnostic is still local or nearly local: staggered
magnetization and spin-spin correlations in the ordering direction.

\section{Our Qubit Hamiltonian}

The Jordan-Wigner transform maps the open Kitaev chain to
\begin{equation}
  H_K
  =
  -\frac{\mu}{2}\sum_{j=0}^{L-1} Z_j
  +\frac{t-\Delta}{2}\sum_{j=0}^{L-2} X_jX_{j+1}
  +\frac{t+\Delta}{2}\sum_{j=0}^{L-2} Y_jY_{j+1}.
  \label{eq:kitaev-qubit}
\end{equation}
This is a transverse-field anisotropic $XY$ chain, not a generic
$Z_jZ_{j+1}$ Ising chain. The spin operators in Eq.~\eqref{eq:kitaev-qubit}
encode fermion occupation and fermion parity. In particular,
\begin{equation}
  n_j=c_j^\dagger c_j=\frac{I-Z_j}{2},
  \qquad
  P=\prod_{j=0}^{L-1}Z_j.
\end{equation}
The $Z$ field is the chemical-potential term, not an external magnetic field
acting on physical spins.

At the sweet spot $t=\Delta$, the $XX$ coupling cancels:
\begin{equation}
  H_K(t=\Delta)
  =
  -\frac{\mu}{2}\sum_j Z_j
  +t\sum_{j=0}^{L-2}Y_jY_{j+1}.
  \label{eq:sweet-spot}
\end{equation}
At $\mu=0$, this becomes a pure nearest-neighbor $YY$ model:
\begin{equation}
  H_K(t=\Delta,\mu=0)
  =
  t\sum_{j=0}^{L-2}Y_jY_{j+1}.
  \label{eq:pure-yy}
\end{equation}
Since $t>0$ in our conventions, each $Y_jY_{j+1}$ bond energetically prefers
opposite $Y$ eigenvalues. In this narrow algebraic sense, the sweet-spot
Hamiltonian is antiferromagnetic in the $Y$ basis.

\section{Why the Similarity Is Limited}

\subsection{The ordering direction is different}

The standard antiferromagnetic Ising chain has an interaction $Z_jZ_{j+1}$ and
therefore orders in the $Z$ basis. Our sweet-spot Hamiltonian has
$Y_jY_{j+1}$ interactions. A single-qubit basis rotation can map $Y$ to $Z$:
\begin{equation}
  R_x\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) Y R_x^\dagger\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right)=Z.
\end{equation}
After such a rotation, Eq.~\eqref{eq:pure-yy} looks like an Ising coupling.
However, this is a basis change inside the qubit encoding. It does not turn the
Majorana system into an ordinary magnetic material.

\subsection{The field term has a fermionic meaning}

In the transverse-field Ising model, the field competes with spin order. In the
Kitaev-chain qubit Hamiltonian, the $Z$ term comes from the chemical potential:
\begin{equation}
  -\mu c_j^\dagger c_j
  \mapsto
  -\frac{\mu}{2}Z_j
  \quad
  \text{up to a constant convention.}
\end{equation}
Changing $\mu$ changes the fermion filling tendency and drives the system
through the topological transition at
\begin{equation}
  |\mu|=2t.
\end{equation}
Thus the same-looking transverse field has a different microscopic origin.

\subsection{The diagnostic is not local staggered magnetization}

An antiferromagnetic Ising phase can be detected by local staggered order:
\begin{equation}
  \langle (-1)^j Z_j\rangle \neq 0
  \quad
  \text{or}
  \quad
  \langle Z_iZ_j\rangle \sim (-1)^{i-j}.
\end{equation}
The Kitaev chain, by contrast, is a topological superconducting phase. In a
fixed-parity eigenstate, single-edge Majorana operators flip parity and have
zero expectation value. The robust observable is a parity-preserving non-local
string, for example
\begin{equation}
  O_{\mathrm{edge}}
  =
  X_0Z_1\cdots Z_{L-2}X_{L-1}.
\end{equation}
For $L=4$, this is
\begin{equation}
  O_{\mathrm{edge}}=X_0Z_1Z_2X_3.
\end{equation}
This is the central difference for the seminar: the Ising antiferromagnet is
organized by conventional symmetry breaking, while the Kitaev phase is
diagnosed through parity, edge-mode degeneracy, and non-local strings.

\section{Gauge and Sign Conventions}

The word ``antiferromagnetic'' depends on both the coupling sign and the chosen
spin basis. For a two-site Pauli coupling
\begin{equation}
  H=J_\alpha\sum_j \sigma_j^\alpha\sigma_{j+1}^\alpha,
\end{equation}
$J_\alpha>0$ favors opposite eigenvalues of $\sigma^\alpha$, while
$J_\alpha<0$ favors equal eigenvalues. In one dimension, some sign changes can
also be moved around by staggered basis rotations. Therefore, calling
Eq.~\eqref{eq:pure-yy} ``antiferromagnetic in the $Y$ basis'' is a statement
about the qubit Hamiltonian's algebraic form, not the main physical content of
the Kitaev phase.

\section{Takeaway}

The sweet-spot Kitaev-chain Hamiltonian resembles an antiferromagnetic Ising
chain after choosing the appropriate spin basis:
\begin{equation}
  H_K(t=\Delta,\mu=0)=t\sum_jY_jY_{j+1}.
\end{equation}
But the project should not present the physics as ordinary antiferromagnetism.
The qubits encode fermions, $Z$ measures local occupation parity, and the
important phase information is not a local staggered magnetization. It is the
topological structure: nearly degenerate parity sectors, Majorana edge modes,
and non-local strings such as $X_0Z_1\cdots Z_{L-2}X_{L-1}$.
